The Touch of Music: Ethnography Project

Exploring how musicians' tactile familiarity with their instruments shapes their identity and self-expression.

Project Overview

This ethnography investigates the relationship between musicians and their instruments, focusing on how touch and familiarity shape their musical identity. Through interviews and observations, we've explored themes such as tactile awareness, emotional connection, mastery, and the impact of instrument familiarity on confidence and performance.

Her journey began in secondary school, influenced by friends who played during recess. She was drawn to the guitar's versatility and ease of learning, finding it a flexible instrument for accompaniment.

Biography and Reflections

Starting in secondary school, she was drawn to guitar by the school’s vibrant music culture. Friends taught her basic chords, sparking a love for the instrument's versatility.

Initially, the steel strings caused discomfort, limiting her playing time. Over time, callouses formed, allowing her to play with ease. She now appreciates the balance between the physical demands of playing and the comfort developed through practice.

Playing guitar requires a unique tactile awareness, especially during fingerpicking and rhythmic tapping, as seen in the video. She describes a conscious physicality when positioning her hands and wrists to play comfortably and effectively.

She likens her guitar to a ‘best friend’ rather than an extension of herself. Her attachment goes beyond functionality; she regularly cleans and cares for her guitar, seeing it as a companion that brings her comfort.

As she hones her guitar skills, she begins to see herself as a musician on the path to mastery. She believes that improving her touch and control will affirm her identity as a guitarist.
