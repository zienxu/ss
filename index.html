The Touch of Music: Ethnography Project

Exploring how musicians' tactile familiarity with their instruments shapes their identity and self-expression.

Project Overview

This ethnography investigates the relationship between musicians and their instruments, focusing on how touch and familiarity shape their musical identity. Through interviews and observations, we've explored themes such as tactile awareness, emotional connection, mastery, and the impact of instrument familiarity on confidence and performance.

Mastery of Touch & Identity Development

Clarinetist

Introduction: A clarinetist with over a decade of experience, Sean views his instrument as a "close work acquaintance," balancing emotional connection with practical mastery.

When the Instrument Becomes a Part of You

"There came a point where it (the clarinet) no longer felt like a foreign object but a part of myself—when I would get lost in the music."

His journey with the clarinet began at a young age, inspired by his father’s playing and driven by his own curiosity. He was captivated by the instrument’s unique sound and complexity, finding it a natural fit despite initial challenges with its size and technique.

Background & Origin

Starting around age eight, inspired by his father, the clarinetist chose the clarinet despite initial encouragement to learn piano. Over the years, he has played on three different clarinets, with his longest-used instrument holding strong emotional significance. Each instrument transition required an adjustment period, reinforcing his connection to familiar setups.

Physical Comfort & Progression

Initially, he faced challenges with the clarinet's size, especially for his left hand, and even added styrofoam layers to the keys for extra support. Over time, handling the instrument became second nature as he advanced to more refined, personalized equipment. Though each upgrade required some adaptation, his control and tactile familiarity with the clarinet have steadily improved, contributing to a deeper sense of comfort.

Tactile Awareness & Physical Interaction

The clarinet offers a deeply tactile experience for him, with each key press providing precise feedback. Unlike instruments requiring approximate placement, the clarinet’s fixed key positions offer him a sense of assurance. His embouchure, or mouth shape, is essential for controlling airflow and tone, with specific notes demanding careful positioning of his tongue and throat. Variations in finger pressure, air speed, and mouth positioning allow him to shift tonal quality and control tuning, making tactile awareness crucial to his playing.

Emotional Bond with the Clarinet and Dependence on Instrument’s Condition

He describes a "love-hate" relationship with his instrument, particularly when using natural reeds due to their variability. Switching to synthetic reeds has stabilized this connection, providing consistent sound. His clarinet feels like a reliable “acquaintance” rather than an extension of himself, a tool treated with respect to achieve optimal performance.

Mastery of Touch & Identity Development

For him, touch mastery includes not only fingers but also embouchure and tongue. When performing emotionally intense pieces like Poulenc’s Sonata, he relies on muscle memory for precision and expressive tonguing techniques. His teacher emphasizes that emotional intention must align with technical skill, making touch mastery essential for conveying emotion.

Violinist

Introduction: A dedicated violinist from age four, Natalie shares a deep emotional connection with her instrument, seeing it as a part of herself and even likening it to a sibling.

Mastering the Art of Soft Touch and Vibrato

"A light, breezy touch with gentle bow pressure for softer sections, enriched with vibrato, brings the piece to life."

Her journey with the violin began within a musically inclined family, where she embraced the instrument with enthusiasm. She chose to keep her teacher’s violin for its unique sound, color, and personal significance, transforming it over the years into a source of joy and self-expression.

Background & Origin

The violinist started playing at age four, motivated by her family’s musical background. She acquired her current violin in primary school, purchasing it from her teacher due to its distinctive tone and color. This violin has since become a cherished instrument, bringing her comfort and allowing her to express herself through music.

Physical Comfort & Muscle Memory

Initially, playing the violin required overcoming challenges in finger placement and coordination. Over years of practice, muscle memory developed, making playing feel natural and fluid, transforming the violin into an extension of herself.

Tactile Awareness & Physical Interaction

Muscle memory allows her to play with minimal conscious thought, though she is still aware of the violin’s weight distribution on her shoulder and fingers. Her right hand, which controls the bow, plays a crucial role in evoking emotions through dynamic adjustments like varying bow pressure and vibrato, conveying moods such as anger or happiness.

Emotional Bond with the Violin

Through years of practice, she has developed a profound connection with her violin, describing it as an additional limb. Her attachment to its specific smell, string tension, and feel provides comfort and reinforces a unique emotional bond. She likens this relationship to that of a sibling, as they have experienced “ups and downs” together over the years.

Mastery of Touch & Identity Development

Touch is essential for her to convey emotion, especially through bow pressure and vibrato speed. Influenced by violinists like Itzhak Perlman and Hilary Hahn, she believes that each musician’s touch shapes their identity. Mood also impacts her playing style, with frustration leading to tense muscles, while a positive mood fosters openness and expressiveness.

Drummer

Introduction: A drummer since age nine, Lucius views his drum set as a reliable "workhorse" that anchors his role in the band’s rhythm and energy.

Fingerpicking and Percussive Rhythms

"At this moment, it felt more dynamic, because I was doing fingerpicking and some percussive movements, so it felt like I had a more rhythmic feel of the guitar rather than if I were just strumming."

After transitioning from classical piano, he found freedom in drumming, where he could explore diverse genres. His current drum set and sticks were chosen for comfort and consistency, helping him perform at his best.

Background & Origin

Starting drums at age nine as a break from classical piano, he found joy in the rhythmic freedom it offered. His current set and sticks were chosen for their balance of flexibility and strength, allowing consistent performance across gigs.

Physical Comfort & Setup

Drum placement, seat height, and pedal alignment are meticulously adjusted for comfort and control, reflecting his attention to detail. His familiarity with his setup enhances his confidence and rhythm, allowing him to focus on the performance.

Tactile Awareness & Control

The drummer relies on precise hand and foot coordination, with subtle grip and angle adjustments impacting the rhythm. This tactile awareness ensures he maintains the beat, balancing technique and emotion to complement the band’s flow.

Emotional Bond with the Drums

He views his drums as a “trusted workhorse,” appreciating their reliability rather than forming a deep emotional bond. His connection is primarily practical, valuing how well his setup supports his skill and role in performances.

Mastery of Touch & Identity Development

For him, touch mastery lies in the subtlety of dynamics. By controlling grip and stroke strength, he conveys emotions like intensity or restraint, shaping his identity as a versatile and reliable drummer who adapts to the energy of each performance.

Electone Player

Introduction: Audrie has been dedicated to her instrument since age seven, discovering a unique musical expression through its blend of traditional and electronic elements. Her connection to the Electone is deeply rooted, viewing it as a reliable ‘buddy’ that supports her artistic journey.

Fingerpicking and Percussive Rhythms

"At this moment, it felt more dynamic, because I was doing fingerpicking and some percussive movements, so it felt like I had a more rhythmic feel of the guitar rather than if I were just strumming."

Her journey with the Electone began at age seven, initially as a practical way to learn music affordably. With its multiple keyboards and pedalboard, the Electone offers her a rich, layered musical experience that she has grown to appreciate more over time. Her mastery of this instrument has allowed her to express complex emotions with subtlety and control.

Background & Origin

She began playing the Electone as a child, introduced by her mother who encouraged her and her sister to develop musical skills. Over 14 years, she has become highly familiar with her Electone, appreciating it not only for its affordability but also for its potential to simulate an orchestra’s range.

Physical Comfort & Adaptation

Mastering the Electone’s dual keyboards and pedalboard took time, especially as she learned to coordinate hands and feet seamlessly. Her own Electone at home is comfortable and familiar, which helps her connect emotionally. In contrast, using her teacher’s Electone with its stiffer keys requires more effort, but this experience has enhanced her adaptability and confidence as a musician.

Tactile Awareness & Dynamic Control

Her sense of touch on the Electone is vital for expressing emotions, especially through the right foot’s control over volume and pitch bends. She finds that being mindful of pressure on each key and pedal allows her to produce subtle shifts in dynamics, helping to convey the emotional nuances of each piece.

Emotional Bond with her Electone

Playing her home Electone brings her into an emotional ‘zone,’ which she likens to an intense form of expression that relieves stress. The Electone has become more than an instrument to her, providing both comfort and an outlet for emotions she can’t express elsewhere. Although she sees it as a ‘friend’ rather than an extension of herself, her Electone plays a key role in shaping her identity as a musician.

Mastery of Touch & Identity Development

Over time, she has refined her touch and control on the Electone, particularly with the pitch bend and dynamic pedals. Her ability to convey both delicate and powerful dynamics has evolved, strengthening her identity and confidence as an Electone player. The familiar touch of her own Electone allows her to perform with greater fluidity, while unfamiliar Electones push her to adjust, deepening her adaptability and skill.
